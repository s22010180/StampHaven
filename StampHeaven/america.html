History Of American Stamps


The history of American stamps dates back to 1847 when the first U.S. postage stamps were issued. Since then, there have been numerous stamps issued for various occasions and purposes, from honoring famous individuals to commemorating significant events.


The first U.S. postage stamps were issued on July 1, 1847, in denominations of 5 and 10 cents. The stamps featured portraits of Benjamin Franklin and George Washington, respectively. At the time, postage was based on the distance the mail traveled, and the cost of sending a letter was paid by the recipient. The introduction of prepaid postage stamps made mailing letters much easier and more efficient.


n the years that followed, the U.S. Post Office Department continued to issue new stamps for various purposes, including commemorating important events, honoring famous individuals, and promoting national causes. In the early 20th century, the use of stamps became more widespread and the designs became more elaborate.


During World War I, the U.S. government issued a series of stamps to help raise funds for the war effort. The stamps featured patriotic images and slogans, and many were sold in souvenir sheets to collectors.


In 1920, the U.S. Post Office Department began issuing stamps for airmail, which had become an increasingly important mode of transportation. These stamps featured images of airplanes and other aviation-related subjects.


Throughout the 20th century, the U.S. Post Office Department and later the U.S. Postal Service issued stamps for a wide range of purposes, including commemorating important anniversaries, promoting tourism, and raising awareness of social issues. In recent years, the Postal Service has also issued stamps featuring popular culture icons such as Harry Potter, Star Wars, and Marvel Comics characters.


In addition to traditional stamps, the U.S. Postal Service has also issued various types of special stamps, including commemorative stamps, which are issued for a limited time to honor a specific event or individual; definitive stamps, which are issued for general use and remain in circulation for an extended period of time; and special issue stamps, which are issued for a particular purpose or cause, such as breast cancer awareness or military veterans.


Today, the U.S. Postal Service continues to issue stamps that reflect the history, culture, and diversity of the United States. Whether used for mailing letters or collected as works of art, U.S. stamps are an enduring symbol of American history and identity.




1847 5-cent Benjamin Franklin Stamp

The first U.S. postage stamp ever issued, featuring a portrait of Benjamin Franklin.

1869 15-cent Lincoln Stamp

A black stamp with a portrait of President Abraham Lincoln, issued to honor his memory after his assassination.

1898 Trans-Mississippi Exposition Stamp

A series of nine stamps commemorating the Trans-Mississippi and International Exposition held in Omaha, Nebraska, in 1898.

1918 Inverted Jenny Stamp

A 24-cent stamp featuring a biplane called a Jenny, with the image printed upside down by mistake. Considered one of the most famous stamp errors.

1922 2-cent Washington Stamp

A stamp featuring a portrait of George Washington that was used extensively for mailing newspapers and periodicals.

1938 Presidential Issue Stamp

A series of 32 stamps featuring portraits of U.S. presidents from George Washington to Franklin D. Roosevelt.

1956 Liberty Issue Stamp

A series of four stamps featuring the Statue of Liberty in different poses.

1962 Project Mercury Stamp

A set of four stamps commemorating the first manned space flights of the Project Mercury program.
