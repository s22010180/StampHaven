History Of British stamps


The first British postage stamps were issued in 1840, just a few months after the first U.S. stamps. The stamps were the famous "Penny Black" and "Two Penny Blue," featuring a portrait of Queen Victoria.


After the Penny Black and Two Penny Blue were issued in 1840, the design of British stamps continued to evolve. In 1841, the Penny Red replaced the Penny Black, featuring a similar design but with a red background instead of black. This was done to make it easier to spot cancellations on the stamp.


Over the years, British stamps have featured many notable designs and innovations. In 1856, the One Shilling Green was issued, which was the first stamp to feature a design in a different color than black. In 1864, the first perforated stamps were issued, making them easier to tear apart and reducing the number of damaged stamps.


In 1870, the first set of commemorative stamps was issued to mark the opening of the Metropolitan District Railway in London. These stamps were only intended to be used for a short time and were then replaced with new definitive stamps. However, commemorative stamps continued to be issued for special occasions such as royal weddings, coronations, and other significant events.


In the 20th century, British stamps continued to feature a wide range of designs, including portraits of famous individuals, images of British landscapes and landmarks, and depictions of cultural icons. One of the most famous British stamps of the 20th century is the "Penny Lilac, " which was issued in 1881 and featured a portrait of Queen Victoria against a lilac background. This stamp was in use for over 30 years and is still widely collected today.


In the years since, British stamps have continued to evolve with the times. In 1967, the Machin Definitives were introduced, featuring a portrait of Queen Elizabeth II designed by Arnold Machin. These stamps are still in use today, with new versions featuring updated designs.


Today, British stamps remain an important part of the country's cultural heritage, with new designs and innovations continuing to be introduced to reflect the changing times. From the early Penny Black to the modern commemorative stamps, British stamps are an enduring symbol of the country's history, culture, and identity.



Penny Black Stamp

The first British postage stamp, featuring a portrait of Queen Victoria and the denomination of one penny. It was issued in May 1840.

Two Penny Blue Stamp

The second British postage stamp, featuring a portrait of Queen Victoria and the denomination of two pence. It was also issued in May 1840.

Penny Red Stamp

A redesign of the Penny Black issued in 1841, with a red background instead of black.

One Shilling Green Stamp

A stamp issued in 1856 featuring a portrait of Queen Victoria and the denomination of one shilling.

Penny Lilac Stamp

A stamp issued in 1881 featuring a portrait of Queen Victoria and a lilac background.

Halfpenny Green Stamp

A stamp issued in 1887 featuring a portrait of Queen Victoria and the denomination of half a penny.

One Pound Seahorse Stamp

A stamp issued in 1913 featuring a portrait of King George V and a design of a seahorse.

Two Pence Blue Mauritius Stamp

A rare stamp issued in 1847 in Mauritius, featuring the words "Post Office" and the denomination of two pence in blue.

Edward VIII 1936 Stamp

A stamp featuring the portrait of King Edward VIII, who abdicated before his coronation in 1936.
